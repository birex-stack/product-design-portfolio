Animation and game design have been a constant thread in my life — from Flash-era interactive experiments to Unity levels I ship solo today.

My role: Solo creator — game design, UX, UI, 3D characters & development

Personal project Unity Mobile Game design AI-assisted

Where it started

I've always been drawn to motion — how things move, react, and feel. Long before product design was my profession, I was building animated Flash sites, interactive presentations, and small game prototypes for the joy of it.

That same instinct shows up in my day job: I think in flows, feedback loops, and moments that feel right. The hobby never stopped — it just waited for the tools to catch up.

What changed

  • AI and modern tooling let one person cover what used to need a team — concept, 3D assets, rigging, UI, and implementation.
  • The constraint isn't imagination anymore — it's iteration speed and taste. That's where years of product design actually help.
  • Hobby projects are my sandbox — companion rosters, combat feel, character personality on a single screen.
  • Shipping something playable end-to-end teaches you what handoffs between design and engineering really cost.

Wolfie Bolts

A mobile action RPG built in Unity — street-art energy,
a pack of wolf companions, and combat you can read at a glance.
Work in progress.

Unlocked wolves worth showing off.

The roster tracks how far you've pushed — each new companion joins with role, signature moves, and attitude on full display. Every unlock is a milestone you can see and flaunt, not another row in a spreadsheet.

The pack fights smarter together.

Each wolf is distinct — different role, different attitude — but they read the fight as a group. Covering each other, stepping in when someone's pinned, moving with the discipline of a pack that trusts its own instincts.

Cinematic moments between the action.

Story beats between stages give the pack room to breathe — cinematics that contrast the arcade energy of combat, deepen the atmosphere, and make each milestone feel worth pushing for.

How I build it

No studio pipeline — a personal stack I iterate with every weekend. The goal is playable levels I'm proud to run — not perfect assets.

  • Concept — pen and paper first. What the game is about, how it should feel, scope and complexity, reference inspirations, and a few defining beats that anchor the vision before anything hits the engine.
  • Unity — game engine, scenes, UI canvas, and mobile builds.
  • Cursor & ChatGPT — faster scripting, prototyping, and problem-solving when I'm stuck.
  • Meshy — 3D character and prop generation, refined by hand in-engine.
  • Adobe Mixamo — rigging and animation for humanoid characters.
  • Affinity — UI graphics, icons, and 2D polish.
  • Korg Wavestate — synthesizer for in-game sounds and musical backdrop.

Why it matters

  • End-to-end ownership From loose idea to playable level — I make the calls and live with the results. That loop is rare in product roles and invaluable for understanding what teams actually ship.
  • Design + implementation in one head No handoff loss between Figma and engine. When a UI element feels wrong, I fix it in the same session — the same instinct I bring to enterprise products, applied to pixels and frame rate.
  • Honest scope for a hobby This isn't a shipped title or professional credit — it's proof that I care deeply about interactive craft, learn fast, and turn ideas into something I genuinely want to play.
